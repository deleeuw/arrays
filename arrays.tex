\section{Decomposition}\label{decomposition}

\subsection{Vectors}\label{vectors}

Suppose \(x\) is an element of \(n\)-dimensional Euclidean space, with
the usual inner product \(x'y\) and the squared norm
\(\|x\|^2:=\sqrt{x'x}\). We can decompose \(x\) into two orthogonal
parts, with the first part a vector with all elements equal to the mean
and the second part a vector with the deviations from the mean. We use a
\(\bullet\) replacing a subscript to indicate averaging over that
subscript. The elementwise decomposition is \begin{equation}
x_i=x_\bullet+(x_i-x_\bullet).\label{eq-onedimelem}
\end{equation}

Equation \eqref{eq-onedimelem} can be written as \begin{equation}
x=Px+(I-P)x,\label{eq-onedimproj}
\end{equation} where \(P\) projects on the subspace of all constant
vectors, i.e.~\(P:=n^{-1}ee'\) with \(e\) a vector with all elements
equal to one. Now \(P^2=P\) and thus \(P(I-P)=0\), which shows that
\(Px\) and \((I-P)x\) are orthogonal. Also, as Pythagoras used to say,
\begin{equation}
\|x\|^2=\|Px\|^2+\|I-Px\|^2.\label{eq-onedimpyth}
\end{equation} Note that equation \eqref{eq-onedimproj} is actually
correct for any square matrix \(P\), but the two components are
orthogonal if and only if \(P\) is a projector. Thus
\eqref{eq-onedimpyth} is also only true if \(P\) is a projector.

We can generalize equation \eqref{eq-onedimproj} to mutually orthogonal
subspaces \(S_1,\cdots,S_m\), with corresponding projectors
\(P_1,\cdots,P_m\). If \(P_1+\cdots+P_m=I\) we can have the orthogonal
decomposition \begin{equation}
x=P_1x+P_2x+\cdots P_mx,
\end{equation} and if \(P_1+\cdots+P_m\lesssim I\) in the Loewner
sense\footnote{The eigenvalues of $P_1+\cdots+P_m$ are less than or equal to one.}
then \begin{equation}
x=P_1x+P_2x+\cdots P_mx+(I-P_1-\cdots-P_m)x
\end{equation} Note that the ``residual'' \(I-P_1-\cdots-P_m\) is
orthogonal to all \(P_1,\cdots,P_m\).

Suppose \(U\) is an \(n\times n\) matrix with \(U'U=nI\) and with first
column equal to \(e\). Define \(\alpha:=n^{-1}U'x\) and
\(P_j:=n^{-1}u_ju_j'\). The \(P_j\) are projectors orthogonal to each
other. Then \begin{equation}
U\alpha=\sum_{j=1}^nP_jx=(\frac{1}{n}ee')x+(I-\frac{1}{n}ee')x=x_\bullet e+(x-x_\bullet).
\end{equation} Although \(\alpha\) depends on the choice of \(U\) the
product \(U\alpha\) is the same for all \(U\).

We can also derive the decomposition from a least squares projection
perspective. Suppose we want to minimize \(\|x-a\|^2\) over all \(a\) of
the form \(a=\alpha e\). The solution for \(\alpha\) is, of course,
\(Px=x_\bullet\). And we may want to minimize \(\|x-a\|^2\) over all
\(a\) satisfying \(e'a=0\), which gives the solution
\((I-P)x=x-x_\bullet\).

The projection formulation suggests some obvious further
generalizations. We can replace the one-dimensional subspace of all
constant vectors by an arbitrary subspace \(S\), and we can replace the
inner product \(x'y\) by an arbitrary inner product \(x'Ay\), with \(A\)
positive semi-definite. If \(H\) is an \(A\)-orthonormal basis for the
subspace \(S\), i.e.~if \(H'AH=I\), then \(Px=HH'Ax\) or \((x-Px)'Az=0\)
for all \(z\in S\).

In the next sections we analyse the same approaches we have used for
vectors to decompositions of linear spaces of matrices and arrays. This
is actually not as much of a generalization as it appears to be, because
matrices and arrays are elements of inner product spaces, just like
vectors are. What we will introduce are different notations and in most
cases different inner products.

\subsection{Matrices}\label{matrices}

Suppose \(X\) is an \(n\times m\) matrix, an element of the space
\(\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\) with inner product
\(\langle X,Y\rangle:=\text{tr}\ X'Y\) and squared norm
\(\|X\|^2=\text{tr}\ X'X\). The elementwise decomposition we are
interested in is \begin{equation}
x_{ij}=x_{\bullet\bullet}+(x_{i\bullet}-x_{\bullet\bullet})+(x_{\bullet j}-x_{\bullet\bullet})+
(x_{ij}-x_{i\bullet}-x_{\bullet j}+x_{\bullet\bullet}).\label{eq-twodimelem}
\end{equation} Using ANOVA terminology we have the \emph{overall
effect}, the two \emph{main effects} for rows and columns, and the
\emph{first order interaction} of the rows and columns.

We define the projectors \begin{subequations}
\begin{align}
P_r&:=n^{-1}ee',\\
P_c&:=m^{-1}ee',
\end{align}
\end{subequations} and with \(Q_r:=I-P_r\) and \(Q_c:=I-P_c\). Equation
\eqref{eq-twodimelem} becomes \begin{equation}
X=P_rXP_c+P_rXQ_c+Q_rXP_c+Q_rXQ_c.\label{eq-twodimproj}
\end{equation} Thus again we have four projectors, orthogonal to each
other, adding up to the identity.

In {de Leeuw} (\citeproc{ref-deleeuw_E_26e}{2026}) we generalize the
results in this section to the inner product
\(\langle X,Y\rangle:=\text{tr}\ RXCY'\), with \(R\) and \(C\) positive
semi-definite. Decomposition \eqref{eq-twodimproj} remains correct, with
\(P_r=ee'R/e'Re\) and \(P_c=Cee'/e'Ce\). Note that both projectors are
asymmetric. Equation \eqref{eq-twodimelem} also remains valid, if we
interpret the bullitt subscripts as weighted means.

To see more clearly how the vector case and the matrix case are
different we use the vec() operator to map \(\mathbb{R}^{n\times m}\)
into \(\mathbb{R}^{nm}\). Using the Kronecker product \(\otimes\) of
matrices we have the identity \begin{equation}
\text{tr}\ RXCY'=x'(C\otimes R)y,
\end{equation} with \(x:=\text{vec}(X)\) and \(y=\text{vec}(Y)\). Thus
our matrix results can be interpreted as a special case of the vector
results in \(\mathbb{R}^{nm}\), with inner product \(x'(C\otimes R)y\).

Define \(U\) and \(V\) to be square matrices with first column equal to
one, \(U'U=nI\) and \(V'V=mI\). Then define \[
\alpha_{ij}=\frac{1}{nm}u_i'Xv_j.
\] Now \[
X=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m\alpha_{ij}u_iv_j'
\] \[
\|X\|^2=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m\sum_{k=1}^n\sum_{l=1}^ma_{ij}a_{kl}\text{tr}\ v_ju_i'u_kv_l'=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m\sum_{k=1}^n\sum_{l=1}^m\delta^{ik}\delta^{jl}a_{ij}a_{kl}=\|A\|^2
\] Term with \(i=1\) and \(j=1\). \[
a_{11}ee'=x_{\bullet\bullet}ee'
\] Terms with \(i=1\) and \(j\neq 1\). \[
\frac{1}{nm}\sum_{j=2}^m\alpha_{1j}ev_j'=\frac{1}{nm}e\sum_{j=2}^m(e'Xv_j)v_j'=n^{-1}ee'XJ_m=e(x_{\bullet_j}-x_{\bullet\bullet})'
\] \[
\frac{1}{nm}\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=2}^m(u_i'Xv_j)u_iv_j'=J_nXJ_m=(x_{ij}-x_{i\bullet}e'-ex_{j\bullet}+x_{\bullet\bullet})
\]
